# Exploratory Data Analysis

## General overview

The dataset contains 419 records documenting timber extraction activities carried out for the Maritime Department of Cádiz between 1748 and 1751, trough direct administration.

The records include information about provinces, municipalities, landownership categories, landowners, tree species, timber quantities and geographical coordinates.

The exploratory analysis combined summary statistics, charts and maps in order to identify the main territorial, environmental and economic patterns associated with forest resource mobilisation for Spanish naval construction.

## Distribution of tree quantities

The histogram of tree quantities shows a highly asymmetric distribution.

Most records correspond to relatively small quantities of trees, while a much smaller number of observations record very large extraction campaigns involving several hundred or even several thousand trees.

This confirms that timber extraction was dominated by a limited number of large extraction campaigns alongside many smaller local fellings.

## Temporal distribution

The analysis of felling periods shows that the greatest quantities of timber were extracted between 1748 and 1750.

The periods with the highest total quantities are:

- 1749–1750: around 19,000 trees
- 1748–1749: around 18,000 trees
- 1750: around 8,100 trees
- 1748–1751: around 6,900 trees
- 1748–1750: around 4,600 trees

The concentration of felling activity between 1748 and 1750 suggests that the most intense phase of forest resource mobilisation coincided with the naval reforms promoted by the Marquis of Ensenada.

## Provincial distribution

The province of Cádiz clearly dominates the dataset and represents almost half of the total recorded tree quantities.

The provincial distribution is approximately:

- Cádiz: 49.7%
- Málaga: 17.9%
- Sevilla: 16.6%
- Huelva: 15.8%

This indicates that the timber supply system was strongly concentrated in western Andalusia, particularly in territories located relatively close to the naval infrastructure of Cádiz and La Carraca.

## Municipal distribution

The municipalities with the highest recorded quantities of trees are:

- Cortes de la Frontera
- Jimena de la Frontera
- Jerez de la Frontera
- Alcalá del Valle
- Ronda
- Grazalema
- Ubrique

These municipalities form a clear cluster in the mountainous and forested areas of Cádiz and Málaga provinces.

Their prominence suggests that inland forest territories in the regions of Campo de Gibraltar, Sierra de Cádiz, Campinas de Jerez and Serranía de Ronda played a decisive role in supplying timber for naval construction.

## Distribution of tree species

Oak species dominate the dataset.

The most important categories are:

- oak
- holm oak
- gall oak
- undefined species
- pine

Oak alone accounts for more than 18,000 recorded trees, followed by holm oak with around 16,000 and gall oak with more than 13,000.

The predominance of oak-related species reflects their strategic importance for shipbuilding because of their strength, durability and suitability for naval construction.

Pine appears in smaller but still significant quantities, while cork oak and olive are only marginally represented.

## Distribution of landownership types

Communal landownership is the most important ownership category in the dataset.

The highest total quantities correspond to:

- communal lands
- noble estates
- undefined ownership
- private property
- ecclesiastical property
- fallow land

Communal lands account for almost 29,000 trees, while noble estates contribute around 16,000.

These figures suggest that the Spanish state depended heavily on communal forests and aristocratic estates in order to obtain timber supplies.

Ecclesiastical properties and assistance institutions appear only marginally in comparison.

The existence of mixed categories such as "private, ecclesiastic" or "private, noble estate" also suggests that some extraction areas involved overlapping forms of ownership.

## Main landowners

The analysis of landowners reveals that a relatively small number of actors controlled a substantial proportion of the recorded timber quantities.

Among the most important landowners are:

- Jerez de la Frontera
- Jimena de la Frontera
- Ronda
- Duque de Arcos
- Marqués de Benamejí
- Duque de Medina Sidonia
- Ubrique
- Pedro Manuel Montezuma
- Cortes de la Frontera

The category "undefined" is the largest group, indicating that many records did not preserve clear information about landownership.

However, the presence of noble families and aristocratic titles among the top contributors confirms the important role of noble estates in the timber economy of eighteenth-century Andalusia.

## Spatial analysis and maps

The maps reinforce the territorial concentration of timber extraction in western Andalusia.

Most felling locations are concentrated in Cádiz, Málaga, Sevilla and Huelva, particularly in inland mountainous areas such as the Serranía de Ronda and the forested territories surrounding Jimena de la Frontera, Cortes de la Frontera, Grazalema and Ronda.

The species maps show that oak, holm oak and gall oak were especially concentrated in the mountainous interior of Cádiz and Málaga, while pine appears in a more dispersed pattern across several provinces.

The landownership maps also reveal important territorial differences.

Communal forests appear across a wide geographical area and represent the most extensive ownership category in the dataset. Noble estates are concentrated in specific inland zones controlled by major aristocratic families, while ecclesiastical lands appear in smaller and more fragmented clusters.

Overall, the maps confirm that timber extraction followed a clear spatial logic linked to forest availability, mountainous environments, transport routes and proximity to the Maritime Department of Cádiz and the naval infrastructure of La Carraca.

## Main conclusions

The exploratory analysis suggests that timber procurement for Spanish naval construction depended on a relatively small number of provinces, municipalities and landowners.

Cádiz was the principal centre of supply, followed by Málaga, Sevilla and Huelva.

The most important extraction areas were located in inland forested territories such as Cortes de la Frontera, Jimena de la Frontera, Jerez de la Frontera and Ronda.

Communal lands and noble estates played the most significant role in timber production.

Oak, holm oak and gall oak were the dominant species, confirming their importance for naval construction.

Overall, the dataset reflects the growing capacity of the eighteenth-century Spanish state to mobilise forest resources across western Andalusia in support of naval expansion and military reform.
